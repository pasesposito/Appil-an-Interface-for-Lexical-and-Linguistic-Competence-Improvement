Lesson FIVE: The Past Simple Tense

Welcome to Lesson Five! In this lesson, we will focus on the Past Simple tense. We’ll learn how to form and use the Past Simple tense to talk about actions that happened in the past. We’ll also cover word formation with suffixes and prefixes, past time expressions like "yesterday" and "last week," and the past forms of common verbs like "go," "have," and "get."

Additionally, you'll learn vocabulary related to daily routines, and we'll gradually progress from learning the rules to applying them in various exercises, culminating in creative writing and personal presentations.

It’s important to understand that the Past Simple tense is often irregular and must be learned as is. Unlike regular verbs, which follow a predictable pattern (adding -ed), irregular verbs do not follow a single rule and need to be memorized individually. This is essential for accurate usage in speaking and writing.

Understanding the Past Simple Tense

The Past Simple tense is used to describe actions that were completed in the past. Here’s how you form it:

  • Affirmative Sentences: Use the past form of the verb. For regular verbs, add -ed to the base form (e.g., "play" becomes "played"). Irregular verbs have unique past forms (e.g., "go" becomes "went").
  • Negative Sentences: Use "did not" (didn't) + base form of the verb. For example, "I did not (didn't) play soccer yesterday."
  • Interrogative Sentences: Use "did" + base form of the verb. For example, "Did you play soccer yesterday?"

Regular vs. Irregular Verbs

When forming the Past Simple tense, verbs can be classified into two categories: regular and irregular.

Regular Verbs

Regular verbs follow a simple pattern: you add -ed to the base form of the verb to create the past form. Here are some examples:

  • Play: "play" becomes "played" (e.g., "I played soccer yesterday.")
  • Work: "work" becomes "worked" (e.g., "She worked late last night.")
  • Watch: "watch" becomes "watched" (e.g., "They watched a movie last weekend.")

Irregular Verbs

Irregular verbs do not follow a consistent pattern, so their past forms need to be memorized. Here are some common examples:

  • Go: "go" becomes "went" (e.g., "I went to the store yesterday.")
  • Have: "have" becomes "had" (e.g., "We had dinner at 8 PM.")
  • Get: "get" becomes "got" (e.g., "She got a new job last month.")

Word Formation: Suffixes and Prefixes

Understanding word formation can help you expand your vocabulary. In English, we often create new words by adding suffixes or prefixes. Here are some examples:

  • Suffixes: Adding -ed to a verb forms the past tense (e.g., "talk" becomes "talked").
  • Prefixes: Adding a prefix can change the meaning of a word (e.g., "undo" is the opposite of "do").

Past Time Expressions

When talking about the past, we often use time expressions to indicate when something happened. Here are some common past time expressions:

  • Yesterday: "I went to the park yesterday."
  • Last week: "She visited her grandmother last week."
  • Two days ago: "They left for vacation two days ago."

Common Verbs in the Past Simple

Here are the past forms of some common verbs:

  • "Go": went
  • "Have": had
  • "Get": got

Practice Transforming Affirmative Sentences into Negative Ones

Transforming affirmative sentences into their negative forms in the Past Simple tense involves using "did not" (or "didn't") followed by the base form of the verb. For example, to change "I went to the market yesterday" into a negative sentence, you would say "I did not go to the market yesterday."
